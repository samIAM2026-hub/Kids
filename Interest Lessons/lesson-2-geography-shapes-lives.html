How Geography Shapes People's Lives

Mountains, rivers, oceans, and weather quietly decide where people live, what they eat, and how they get around. Let's uncover the hidden rules.

⏱️ 45–60 min 🎯 Beginner–Intermediate 📍 Vancouver examples
1 The big idea

Why we're here

Main learning goal: See that geography isn't just a backdrop — it actively shapes how people live. By the end you'll be able to explain why people settle where they do, and how climate, landforms, water, and natural resources affect food, housing, transportation, jobs, and culture.
☔ Climate🏔️ Mountains & rivers🌊 Oceans ⛏️ Natural resources🏘️ Why people live where they do🍽️ Food, jobs & culture
2 In plain words

Places set the rules; people play the game

Imagine two families — one lives high in the mountains, one lives on a warm coast. Without anyone telling them to, their lives turn out very differently. The mountain family might raise animals and build steep-roofed houses to shed snow. The coast family might fish, trade by boat, and never own a snow shovel.

Nobody chose those differences on purpose. The land and weather nudged them. That's the heart of this lesson: geography gives people opportunities (a river to fish, flat land to farm) and challenges (a mountain to cross, a desert with no water), and people build their whole way of life around them.

📍 Vancouver connection Vancouver grew where it did for a very geographic reason: a deep, sheltered natural harbour (Burrard Inlet) where big ships can safely dock. Add a mild climate and a railway that needed a Pacific endpoint, and a city was almost guaranteed to appear right here.
👧 Little Explorer · age ~9

Where you live changes how you live! People by the sea might eat fish and travel by boat. People where it snows wear big warm coats.

In Vancouver we have mountains AND the ocean. So we get lots of rain ☔, we can go skiing ⛷️, and big ships 🚢 bring us toys and food. The land helps decide what we eat, wear, and do every single day!

5 Do it yourself

Map Activity: Where Would YOU Build a City?

🗺️ The City-Planner Challenge

Open Google Maps in Satellite view (or use a printed map of BC / Canada). You're a city planner looking for the perfect spot for a brand-new town of 100,000 people.

  1. Explore the map and find three possible spots. For each, note: Is there fresh water nearby? Is the land flat enough to build on? Is it easy to reach (coast, river, or valley)?
  2. Pick your winner and write one sentence explaining the geography behind your choice.
  3. Now zoom to real Vancouver. Which of your “rules” did the real city follow? (Look for the harbour, the flat delta land, and the Fraser River.)
  4. Compare: find a place where a city would be hard to build — the middle of the Rocky Mountains, or the far northern tundra — and say why.

Finish line: Draw your winning site as a simple map with a harbour or river, flat building land, and a road or rail line in or out.

👧 Age 9, make it easier: Just find one good spot for a pretend town on the map. Look for water and flat land, then tell us why you picked it!
6 Talk it through

Discussion Questions

  • Vancouver is one of the most expensive cities in Canada to buy a home. How might mountains and ocean — which leave less flat land to build on — be part of the reason?
  • Salmon is a huge part of food, jobs, and Indigenous culture on the BC coast. How is a single animal tied to geography?
  • Why do people in Vancouver own rain jackets while people in Calgary (just over the mountains) own more snow gear? What's different about the geography?
  • If your family suddenly moved to a hot desert country, name three things about daily life that would have to change.
🧒 Easier questions for our age-9 explorer
  • Why do we wear rain boots and rain jackets a lot in Vancouver?
  • What yummy food comes from the ocean near us?
  • Would you rather live by the sea or on a mountain? Why?
🚀 Challenge questions (for advanced explorers)
  • The mountains east of Vancouver create a “rain shadow” — one side is wet, the other side dry. Can you explain, using clouds and mountains, why the Interior of BC (like the Okanagan) is so much drier and sunnier?
  • Many countries with lots of oil or minerals are wealthy — but some stay poor. Why might having valuable natural resources not always make a country's people rich?

8 Take it home

Mini Project: The Geography Detective

⏱️ 30 min🔍 Investigate🏡 Do at home

Solve “Why do people here live the way they do?”

Pick Vancouver or any place your family has lived or visited. Become a geography detective and find four clues that connect the land to daily life.

  • 🍽️ Food: Name one food the geography helps provide (e.g., BC salmon or blueberries).
  • 🏠 Housing: How does climate or land affect the homes? (Sloped roofs? Tall condos because land is limited?)
  • 🚗 Transportation: Ferries? SeaBus? Mountain highways? Why those?
  • 🎉 Jobs or culture: A job, festival, or tradition tied to the place (fishing, skiing, forestry, the ocean).

Write your four clues as a short “case file,” then finish with one sentence: “Life here is shaped mostly by ___.”

👧 Age 9 version: Draw one food, one home, and one way to travel that fits Vancouver — like a salmon 🐟, a tall condo 🏢, and a ferry ⛴️.
9 For the grown-ups

Parent / Teacher Guidance

🧑‍🏫 How to teach Lesson 2

Goal in one line: Move from “what is geography” to “geography does something.” Success looks like the child spontaneously connecting a physical feature to how people live.

Pacing (about 55 min): ~10 min on sections 2–3, ~15 min on the City-Planner map activity (the standout), ~10 min discussion, ~10 min quiz, project sent home.

Anchor everything locally. Vancouver is a gift for this lesson — harbour, mountains, rain, salmon, ferries, expensive housing. Kids already have opinions about these; connect those opinions to geography.

The rain-shadow challenge is a great whiteboard moment: draw a mountain, wet clouds rising and dropping rain on the near side, and dry air sinking on the far side. It explains the wet Coast vs. sunny Okanagan in one sketch.

Keep it non-deterministic. Geography influences life, it doesn't 100% decide it — people also make choices and use technology. If a sharp kid pushes back with “but people build cities in deserts too,” celebrate it; that's exactly the nuance (section on resources and the challenge question explore it).

Watch for: treating the links as one-way facts to memorize. The real skill is tracing the chain themselves, so ask “and then what does that lead to?” repeatedly.

👧 Doing it with a 9-year-old too? The younger child usually loves this lesson most — it's about food, rain, boats, and salmon. Read the pink Little Explorer box aloud, let her answer the 🧒 questions and the Little Explorer Quiz, and give her the “find one town spot” version of the map game while the older one does the full city-planner challenge.
