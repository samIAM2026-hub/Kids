Geography & Today's World

Trade, migration, growing cities, disasters, and climate change all have geography hiding underneath. Learn to read the news like a geographer.

⏱️ 45–60 min 🎯 Intermediate 🌍 Global + Vancouver examples
1 The big idea

Why we're here

Main learning goal: Use geography to make sense of today's world — trade, migration, cities, the environment, natural disasters, and climate change — and to understand the news and global events. By the end you'll be able to look at a headline and ask “where is this, and how does geography explain it?”
🚢 Trade🧳 Migration🏙️ Growing cities 🌋 Natural disasters🌡️ Climate change🗞️ Reading the news
2 In plain words

The news is geography in action

Almost every big story on the news — a shipping delay, a wildfire, people moving to a new country, a flood, a heat wave — has a where at its centre. Geography is the secret decoder. Once you learn to ask “where is this happening, and why there?”, world events stop feeling random and start making sense.

In our connected world, places are also linked more than ever. A drought in one country can raise food prices in another. A factory in Asia can put a toy on a shelf in Vancouver. That web of connections — goods, people, weather, and ideas moving across the map — is what makes geography so useful right now.

📍 Vancouver connection In the summer of 2021, a record heat wave and later serious flooding hit BC. Highways and rail lines were cut, and store shelves in Vancouver briefly ran low. That's geography, climate, transportation, and trade all colliding in one local story.
👧 Little Explorer · age ~9

The whole world is connected! A toy might be made far, far away and travel to us on a big ship 🚢 across the ocean.

People also move here from other countries — that's why Vancouver has friends and families from all over the world 🌍. And when there's a big fire or flood somewhere, it can even change things for people far away. Everything is joined up!

5 Do it yourself

Map Activity: Track a Journey Across the World

🚢 Follow the Stuff (and the People)

Use Google Maps (or a printed world map). Pick one mission:

  • Mission A — Trace your stuff: Find a “Made in ___” label on a toy, phone, or piece of clothing at home. Locate that country on the map. Draw the route it likely travelled across the ocean to reach the Port of Vancouver, then to your house. How many oceans and countries did it cross?
  • Mission B — Trace a journey: Vancouver is a city built by migration. Pick a country many Vancouverites have family in (for example China, India, the Philippines, or the UK). Map the route someone might travel to move here, and list two geography challenges along the way (oceans, distance, mountains).
  1. Mark the start and end with dots and draw the route between them.
  2. Label the oceans and at least two countries the route passes near.
  3. Write one sentence: “Geography made this journey ___ (easy / hard / possible) because ___.”
👧 Age 9, make it easier: Find one “Made in ___” label at home, then find that country on the map. Wow — look how far it travelled to reach you!
6 Talk it through

Discussion Questions

  • Vancouver's port is Canada's busiest, connecting the country to Asia. Why is being on the Pacific coast such a big economic advantage for Canada?
  • Island countries like the Maldives or Tuvalu are worried about rising sea levels. Why does geography put them in more danger from climate change than a mountain country?
  • BC wildfire smoke sometimes drifts hundreds of kilometres and reaches Vancouver's sky. What does this show about how connected places are?
  • People move for many reasons — jobs, safety, family, climate. If you had to move your family to another country, what geography would you look for first?
🧒 Easier questions for our age-9 explorer
  • Where did one of your toys come from? Let's check the label!
  • Do you know a country your family or friends came from?
  • Have you ever seen smoky orange skies from wildfires?
🚀 Challenge questions (for advanced explorers)
  • Africa is a huge, resource-rich continent, yet many of its countries were shaped by borders drawn by faraway European powers. How might borders that ignore geography and people cause problems even today?
  • As the Arctic warms, sea ice is melting and new shipping routes are opening across the top of the world. Who might benefit, who might worry, and why is Canada especially interested?

8 Take it home

Mini Project: Geographer in the Newsroom

⏱️ 30–40 min🗞️ Research + map🏡 Do at home

Explain a real news story using geography

With a grown-up's help, find one recent news story that involves a place — a wildfire, a flood, a shipping story, people moving, or a weather event (local BC news is perfect).

  • 🗺️ Locate it: Find the place on a map and note the continent, country, and any nearby ocean, mountains, or rivers.
  • 🔎 Explain it: Write 3–4 sentences on how geography helps explain the story (Why there? Who else is affected?).
  • 🔗 Connect it: Does this event reach Vancouver or Canada in any way — through trade, weather, or people?
  • 🎨 Show it: Draw a small map marking the place and one arrow showing how it connects somewhere else.

Present it as a one-page “Geographer's News Report.”

👧 Age 9 version: Pick a news picture of a place with a grown-up, find it on the map, and draw one arrow showing how it connects to us in Vancouver.
9 For the grown-ups

Parent / Teacher Guidance

🧑‍🏫 How to teach Lesson 3

Goal in one line: Turn geography into a lens for current events. Success looks like the child pointing at a news story and naming the geography inside it.

Pacing (about 60 min): ~12 min on sections 2–4, ~15 min on the journey map activity, ~10 min discussion, ~10 min quiz, ~10+ min to start the newsroom project (finished at home).

Choose news carefully. For a 12-year-old, favour place-and-weather stories (wildfires, floods, shipping, a sports team travelling, a food shortage) over frightening or graphic events. Keep it curious, not scary — you're building understanding, not anxiety.

Use the local hooks. BC gives natural material: wildfire smoke, the 2021 heat dome and floods, the Port of Vancouver, and Vancouver's incredibly diverse, migration-built neighbourhoods. Kids connect faster when the example is their own city.

Keep balance on big topics. Climate change and migration can be sensitive. Stick to the geography (where, why, who's affected, how places connect) and present it evenhandedly, letting the child form their own views.

Then do the capstone below — the final review pulls all three lessons together into one project. It's the best single measure of whether the course landed.

👧 Doing it with a 9-year-old too? This lesson touches wildfires, floods, and migration — keep her version gentle and concrete (toys travelling on ships, family from other countries, smoky skies) rather than scary. Read the pink Little Explorer box aloud, let her do the 🧒 questions and Little Explorer Quiz, and pair the two of them on the capstone: younger draws, older writes.

★ Bring it all together

Final Review: The Vancouver Geography Portfolio

This capstone connects all three lessons. It's a mini-project that proves you can think like a geographer — from the basics, to how places shape lives, to today's world.

🏆 Your Mission: A 4-Page “Story of a Place”

Pick one place — Vancouver is the easiest and richest choice — and make a short portfolio (drawn, typed, or slides). One page per part:

1
Map it (Lesson 1).

Draw or print a map of your place with a title, compass rose, legend, and both a physical feature and a human feature labelled.

2
Explain the life (Lesson 2).

Show how the geography shapes food, housing, transportation, and jobs/culture there. Use the “chain reaction” idea: one physical fact → three effects on people.

3
Connect to the world (Lesson 3).

Draw one arrow to somewhere else on Earth — a trade route, a migration path, or a shared climate risk — and explain the connection.

4
Big finish.

Write 3–4 sentences answering: “Everything about this place comes back to one geographic idea, which is ___.” Then read it aloud to your family.

✅ If you can do all four, you've mastered the whole course: what geography is, how it shapes lives, and how it explains today's world.

👧🧭 Two ages, one team: the younger explorer (age ~9) can draw the maps and pictures while the older one (age ~12) writes the sentences and the world connection. One portfolio, made together!

🎓 Course completion Finished all three quizzes and the portfolio? You're officially a Junior Geographer. The best geographers never stop asking “where — and why there?”
