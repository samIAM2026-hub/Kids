What Is Geography?

Geography isn't about memorizing capitals. It's the story of where things are — and why that matters for people, nature, and everyday life.

⏱️ 45–60 min 🎯 Beginner 📍 Vancouver examples
1 The big idea

Why we're here

Main learning goal: Understand that geography is a way of asking “where is it, and why there?” — a tool for understanding people, places, nature, culture, the economy, and world events. By the end you'll be able to explain the difference between physical and human geography and describe how maps help us make sense of the world.

2 In plain words

Geography = the “where” and the “why there”

Every place on Earth has two kinds of stories. One is about nature — the mountains, rivers, oceans, and weather. The other is about people — where they live, what they eat, how they get around, and the languages they speak. Geography connects the two.

Think about the exact spot you're sitting in right now. Geography asks: Why is a city here and not 100 km north? Why does it rain so much in one place and almost never in another? Why do people in one country grow rice while another grows wheat? None of that is random — and geography helps us figure out the reasons.

📍 Vancouver connection Vancouver sits between the ocean and the mountains. That single fact explains a huge amount: the busy port, the rainy winters, the ski hills you can see from downtown, and why the city can't just sprawl endlessly in every direction. Geography is already shaping your day.
👧 Little Explorer · age ~9

Geography is just a big word for learning about places. It asks two fun questions: “Where is it?” and “Why is it there?”

Look outside! The mountains, the ocean, the rain, the houses and roads — geography is the story of ALL of it. In Vancouver we have big mountains AND the sea, so that's why we get lots of rain and can go to the beach and the ski hill. Cool, right?

3 Two halves of one subject

Physical vs. Human Geography

Geographers split their subject into two big teams. Most interesting questions actually need both.

🏔️ Physical Geography

The natural world — the parts of Earth that would be here with or without people.

  • Mountains, rivers, coastlines, forests
  • Climate and weather (rain, snow, heat)
  • Oceans, lakes, earthquakes, volcanoes
  • Plants and animals in a region

🏙️ Human Geography

The human world — how people use and change the places they live.

  • Cities, towns, and where people settle
  • Food, jobs, transportation, housing
  • Languages, cultures, and traditions
  • Trade, borders, and countries
💡 Try it “Stanley Park has a rainforest” = physical. “Millions of people visit Stanley Park each year on the seawall” = human. Same place, two lenses.

5 The geographer's superpower

How Maps Help Us Understand the World

A map is a picture of a place from above, shrunk down so we can see the whole thing at once. Good maps do three jobs: they show us where things are, how far apart they are, and what a place is like.

🧭 Map skill Every map has a compass rose (which way is north), a scale (how much real distance a small map-distance stands for), a legend/key (what the symbols and colours mean), and a title (what the map is about). Find all four the next time you open one.
6 Do it yourself

Map Activity: Zoom Out from Your Front Door

🛰️ The Google Maps Zoom Game

You'll need a phone, tablet, or computer with Google Maps (or a printed map + a globe works too).

  1. Search your home address in Vancouver. Zoom in until you can see your street and your roof.
  2. Now zoom out one step at a time. After each step, say out loud what new thing you can see: your neighbourhood → the city → Metro Vancouver → British Columbia → Canada → North America → the whole Earth.
  3. Switch to Satellite view. Spot one physical feature (the mountains, the Fraser River, the ocean) and one human feature (a bridge, the airport, downtown towers).
  4. Find the compass and the scale bar on screen. Which direction is the ocean from your home — N, S, E, or W?

Finish line: Draw a simple “nested squares” diagram — a tiny square (your home) inside bigger squares for city, province, country, continent, planet. Label each one.

👧 Age 9, make it easier: Just do steps 1 and 2 together — zoom in to find our home, then zoom out slowly and shout out each new thing you see (“our street! … our city! … the ocean! … the whole Earth!”).
7 Talk it through

Discussion Questions

  • If a friend from another country asked “What is Vancouver like?”, what three things would you tell them — and are they physical or human geography?
  • Why do you think almost every big city in the world is built next to water (an ocean, a lake, or a river)?
  • Maps leave things out on purpose. What might a map of Vancouver choose not to show, and why?
  • Is a globe or a flat map better? When would you use each one?
🧒 Easier questions for our age-9 explorer
  • Point with your hand: which way is the ocean from our home?
  • What's your favourite place outside? Is it made by nature or by people?
  • Can you name one animal that lives near Vancouver?
🚀 Challenge questions (for advanced explorers)
  • A flat map of the whole Earth always stretches or squishes something. Why is it impossible to flatten a round Earth perfectly? (Hint: try flattening an orange peel.)
  • Latitude and longitude are like a giant grid on Earth. Vancouver is near 49°N latitude. What do you think you'd find if you travelled all the way around the world staying on that same line?

9 Take it home

Mini Project: My Place on Earth

⏱️ 20–30 min✏️ Drawing + writing🏡 Do at home

Make a one-page “Where I Live” map poster

On a blank sheet, draw a map of your neighbourhood from above (a bird's-eye view). You don't need it to be perfect — geographers sketch all the time.

  • Include your home, your school, and one park or store you know.
  • Add a compass rose, a simple legend (at least 3 symbols), and a title.
  • Colour green for parks/trees, blue for water, and grey for roads.
  • Write two sentences: one physical-geography fact about your area and one human-geography fact.

Bonus: Give your map to a family member and see if they can find your home using only your legend and compass.

👧 Age 9 version: Draw a bird's-eye map of just your bedroom or our home — bed, door, window, toys. Add a sun in one corner to show which way is up, and label three things. (Big sister can help with the compass and legend!)
10 For the grown-ups

Parent / Teacher Guidance

🧑‍🏫 How to teach Lesson 1

Goal in one line: Help the child see geography as a way of thinking (“where + why”), not a list to memorize. If they finish the lesson asking “why is this here?” about things around them, it worked.

Pacing (about 50 min): ~10 min reading and chatting through sections 2–4, ~15 min on the Google Maps zoom activity (this is the heart of the lesson — don't rush it), ~10 min discussion, ~10 min quiz, and the mini project sent home.

Make it a conversation. The best moments come from their own examples. When they name something, gently ask “is that physical or human?” Both answers are often defensible — that's a feature, not a bug.

If they're stuck on physical vs. human: use the test “Would this still be here if no people existed?” Yes → physical. No → human.

For advanced kids: the orange-peel demo (section 7) is worth actually doing — peel an orange, try to flatten the peel, and watch it tear. It's the clearest way to feel why world maps distort.

Watch for: the idea that “geography = capital cities.” Keep steering back to reasons and connections. Praise good questions as much as right answers.

👧 Doing it with a 9-year-old too? Read the main text with the older child, then read the pink Little Explorer box aloud for the younger one. Let the 9-year-old answer the 🧒 questions and the tap-to-reveal Little Explorer Quiz, while the 12-year-old takes the full quiz. Pair them up on the activity — younger draws, older labels — so they finish one map together.
